Change management models
Introduction

In organizational change management, approaches to driving change are commonly classified into two broad categories: top-down and bottom-up. Each approach has its benefits and challenges and is applicable based on the organization’s structure, culture, and the nature of the change being implemented. While some change management models fit neatly into one of these categories, others can be adapted to either, depending on how they are applied. 

In this reading, you will discover eight well-known change management models, each categorizing them as either top-down or bottom-up. You will also discuss those that are adaptable to both approaches.

What are change management models?

Change management models are structured frameworks that guide organizations through transitions, typically categorized into top-down or bottom-up approaches. 

In a top-down approach, senior leadership drives the change, providing clear direction, control, and alignment with strategic goals, making it effective for large-scale or rapid transformations. 

Bottom-up approaches, on the other hand, involve employees at all levels, fostering engagement, innovation, and ownership by empowering staff to identify and implement improvements. 

Some models, like Bridge's Transition Model, are adaptable to both approaches, allowing flexibility based on organizational needs for more effective and sustainable change.

Top-down change management models

Top-down change management models, led by senior management, are ideal for rapid, large-scale change. They provide clear leadership direction, ensuring alignment with strategic goals. By centralizing control, these models streamline decision-making, enforce accountability, and ensure consistency across the organization. Top-down approaches maintain focus, minimize resistance, and accelerate progress during critical transitions. They allow leadership to retain control over the direction and outcomes of the change, making them useful for organizations needing swift alignment across functions and regions. The following models exemplify top-down approaches in change management.

1. Lewin’s Change Management Model

Kurt Lewin’s model is a well-established framework divided into three phases:

Unfreeze

Change

Refreeze

In the Unfreeze phase, leadership prepares the organization by creating awareness of the need for change and disrupting the status quo. The Change phase involves implementing new behaviors or processes, while the Refreeze phase ensures these changes become ingrained in the organizational culture. Leadership is central throughout, particularly in the Unfreeze phase, where senior management drives the articulation and motivation for change. 

Lewin’s model exemplifies a top-down approach, relying heavily on leadership to guide, manage, and sustain the change process at all levels.

2. Kotter’s 8-Step Change Model

John Kotter’s 8-Step Change Model is another prominent top-down change management framework. It outlines eight distinct steps: 

Establishing a sense of urgency

Creating a guiding coalition

Developing a vision and strategy

Communicating the vision

Empowering broad-based action

Generating short-term wins

Consolidating gains

Anchoring new approaches in the culture

Each step requires strong leadership engagement. For example, establishing a sense of urgency and forming a guiding coalition are typically led by senior leaders. By creating a guiding coalition, leaders ensure they have the right people supporting the vision, which cascades through the organization. The entire model revolves around leadership driving the change process, ensuring alignment and buy-in. Kotter’s model emphasizes the critical role of vision-setting and communication, making it a prime example of a top-down approach.

3. McKinsey 7-S Framework

The McKinsey 7-S Framework focuses on aligning seven key organizational elements:

Strategy: The organization's plan to achieve long-term goals

Structure: The hierarchy or arrangement of teams

Systems: Processes and procedures guiding daily operations

Shared Values: Core beliefs and principles that influence the organization's culture

Style: Leadership approach and management style

Staff: The workforce and their roles

Skills: Employee capabilities and competencies

Senior leadership plays a pivotal role in ensuring alignment across these elements, as strategic decisions made by top management affect every part of the organization. Leaders must ensure that each element supports the overarching strategy. For instance, aligning the right skills and staff with the desired strategy requires strong leadership to execute change smoothly across all seven elements. The McKinsey 7-S model exemplifies a top-down approach by emphasizing leadership’s role in managing these interconnected elements.

4. ADKAR Model (Prosci)

The ADKAR Model, developed by Prosci, stands for Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, and Reinforcement. It focuses on managing individual change. While it emphasizes the individual’s experience, it requires strong leadership to ensure that each phase is effectively implemented across the organization.

Leadership is responsible for creating awareness and desire for change, providing the knowledge and resources needed for employees to develop the required skills, and reinforcing the change to ensure sustainability. Although the ADKAR model centers on individuals, it is driven by leadership, making it another example of a top-down model. Leaders must ensure the change aligns with organizational goals and cascades effectively.

Bottom-up change management models

Bottom-up change management models take a different approach by focusing on change initiated at the grassroots level. These models foster employee engagement, innovation, and ownership as employees take an active role in the change process. Bottom-up models create an environment where employees feel empowered to identify problems, propose solutions, and implement changes. They are particularly valuable when organizations seek to leverage the collective knowledge and creativity of their workforce to drive incremental improvements. The following models are categorized as bottom-up approaches:

1. Kaizen (Continuous improvement)

Kaizen, a Japanese term meaning "continuous improvement," is based on the idea that small, incremental changes can lead to significant improvements over time. This model relies on employees to identify areas for improvement in day-to-day operations and propose solutions.

Kaizen thrives on employee involvement, where staff at all levels participate in identifying inefficiencies and suggesting improvements. Management provides support and resources, but the change is largely employee-driven. Kaizen is especially effective in dynamic industries where ongoing innovation is critical.

2. Lean Change Management

Lean Change Management combines principles from Lean and Agile methodologies, focusing on iterative, small-scale changes rather than large-scale transformations. It emphasizes rapid experimentation, constant feedback, and incremental improvement, with employees playing a key role in driving these changes. In this model, employees are empowered to experiment and implement small changes with minimal top-down oversight. Leadership acts as a facilitator rather than a director, fostering collaboration and a culture of continuous improvement. Lean Change Management is a bottom-up model that thrives on employee-driven innovation.

3. Participative Management

Participative Management emphasizes collaboration between employees and management. Employees contribute ideas and participate in decision-making processes, fostering ownership and engagement in organizational changes. In this model, change is driven by employees working closely with management to shape the organization’s future. This bottom-up approach ensures that employees have a voice in how changes are implemented, increasing buy-in and minimizing resistance. Participative Management is particularly effective in cultures that value inclusivity and teamwork, allowing for more sustainable change.

Change management models adaptable to both approaches

Some models can be adapted to either top-down or bottom-up approaches, depending on how they are implemented. These models offer flexibility and can be customized to fit an organization’s specific needs.

1. Bridge’s Transition Model

William Bridge’s Transition Model focuses on the psychological aspects of change, breaking it down into three stages: 

Ending

Neutral Zone

New Beginning

While the model addresses how individuals experience change, it can be applied as either a top-down or bottom-up approach. In a top-down approach, leadership guides employees through each stage, preparing them for the "ending" of the current state and leading them toward the "new beginning." Alternatively, employees can take ownership of their transitions, with leadership offering support. The Transition Model’s flexibility allows organizations to decide whether the transition should be leadership-driven or employee-led.

2. Nudge Theory

Nudge Theory, developed by Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein, suggests that small, positive reinforcements ("nudges") can influence behavior without imposing directives. Leadership can use nudges to encourage desired behaviors across the organization, making it a top-down approach. However, employees can also nudge one another toward new behaviors, making it adaptable as a bottom-up model. Nudge Theory can be used when leaders design environments that subtly steer employees toward preferred behaviors or when employees nudge each other toward collective change.

Hybrid approach: Combining top-down and bottom-up models

Many organizations find that a hybrid approach—combining both top-down and bottom-up models—offers the best results. This approach allows leadership to set strategic goals while engaging employees to foster innovation and ownership, creating a balanced and inclusive change process.

View this approach through the scenario of TechInnovate and how the hybrid approach suits them best.

Scenario

TechInnovate, a leading technology company, is experiencing rapid growth and expansion into new markets. To support this growth, the company is implementing a variety of change initiatives, including:

A restructuring of its management hierarchy (structural change).

The adoption of advanced project management software (technological change).

A shift to a more people-centric culture to boost employee engagement (people-centric change). 

TechInnovate’s senior leadership directs the strategic vision for digital transformation, while employees are encouraged to contribute insights and ideas to improve processes. This hybrid model ensures that the transformation aligns with organizational goals while fostering creativity and engagement.

Conclusion

In this reading, you learned that change management models can be classified as either top-down or bottom-up. Top-down models like Lewin’s and Kotter’s provide strong leadership direction, while bottom-up models like Kaizen encourage employee-driven innovation. Flexible models, such as Bridge’s Transition Model, can be adapted to suit either approach. 

A hybrid model, combining both top-down and bottom-up elements, often leads to the most effective and sustainable change, as demonstrated by TechInnovate’s successful transformation efforts. This balanced approach helps organizations navigate change more effectively and achieve lasting success.
